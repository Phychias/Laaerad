    Major Components¶

    The Trusk Empire was primarily composed of three major kingdoms: index, also known as the Northern Land Kingdom, mainly consisting of the territories of the nomadic Glömezher tribes in the north; index, the core territory of the empire, also known as the Heimet Kingdom, later divided into the Heimet and Tyesuku kingdoms; Laprio, also known as the Novaya Kingdom. "Novaya" means "seaside" in ancient Laprio language.

    Origins: The Beginning of the Artisan Nation¶

    Trusk City-States: The Establishment of Civilization¶

    According to mythology, the Trusk tribes should also have migrated down from the Lakme Saumen region to the Trusk Basin and surrounding areas.

    Including the Trusk city-state, there were many city-states of various sizes within the Trusk region, scattered among the complex hills of the Trusk Basin. Most of these city-states originated from cities near rivers, gradually expanding their respective boundaries until their territories began to overlap. The territory of Trusk that we speculate today included rich mineral mountains and rivers from which fragmented ore could be panned. This time is too far from the present, and there are no very clear historical records. However, considering that detailed records of metal forging already existed during the subsequent Kuileot Dynasty, Trusk at that time—as their given name suggests—should have been quite skilled at forging metal. This is also considered the main reason for Trusk's rise and eventual dominance.

    Besides Trusk, there were many other city-states within the basin, the most important of which was probably Bagunalo. Their territory was mainly concentrated around the largest freshwater lake in the Trusk region, quite close to the core of the Trusk city-state. The prototype of the God Taciap among the six main gods likely originated from this city-state. They also had a long history of friction with the Trusk city-state, ultimately succumbing to Trusk's superior weapons and soldiers. After conquering Bagunalo, the largest grain-producing area in Trusk, military supplies were no longer a problem for Trusk. Several nearby city-states surrendered one after another, while more distant tribes and city-states maintained a wait-and-see attitude. Given Trusk's transportation level at the time, the range Trusk could effectively control was probably relatively limited. After being conquered, Bagunalo ceased to exist as a political unit but was remembered and preserved as a place name.

    After gaining control of several surrounding city-states, the Trusk people seemed to have lost the use for their forging technology for a time. However, they soon turned to technological innovation. Based on archaeological discoveries, it is speculated that they may have begun using primitive mechanical components such as pulleys and iron chains. Building on this, the Trusk people launched a large-scale land reclamation movement and systematically constructed infrastructure such as canals and roads within the territory of what is now the New Trusk city-state. These construction achievements quickly gave them the capability for long-distance expeditions, ultimately expanding their rule to the entire Trusk Basin.

    The First King: KuileotKuileot¶

    In contemporary Truskean, Kuileot not only exists as a surname but has also evolved into KuilemKuilem, a term for high-ranking nobles. There are various legends about the origin of King Kuileot's name. The first hypothesis suggests that an early city-state conquered by the Trusk city-state worshipped a deity named Kuileot. During the long process of integration, Kuileot gradually evolved into a general term for the nobles of that city-state. However, the current Trusk-Heimet pantheon does not include the name Kuileot. Therefore, another theory states that the word originally meant "gold" in ancient Truskean—Kuileot might have been a general term for merchants traveling between city-states at the time. These merchants were accustomed to using gold for transactions and carried gold objects, hence the name; some of these merchants later became strategists, playing an important role in Trusk's wars to unify the northwest of the empire. The "gold" hypothesis has another explanation: based on inscription research, this might have been an ancient honorific for the king, symbolizing that he possessed the most gold in the world. In summary, Kuileot was a general term for a highly influential political force during the ancient Trusk city-state era, and this force eventually coalesced into King Kuileot.

    Kuileot is the first recorded monarch of Trusk. Before him, Trusk seems not to have formed a tradition of recording history. Although the content of unearthed inscriptions is quite detailed, major events might have occurred earlier, leading to the destruction of all early royal inscriptions. Inscriptions record that during his reign, Kuileot gathered treasures from the world, issued decrees, built palaces, and mentioned wars with Heimet, but did not detail the specific circumstances of the battles. Judging from the inscription's record that "treasures were nearly exhausted," this war should have been quite brutal. Ultimately, Kuileot successfully conquered Heimet, becoming the overlord of all city-states along the two great rivers from Trusk to the strait.

    Several monarchs who succeeded Kuileot also left inscriptions, recording the economic and political conditions of the time, although the content is relatively brief. It is worth noting that the names of these early successors are not related to Kuileot; one was even named "Liekukya" (meaning "the one who roasts meat"). However, starting from the fourth monarch, all royal titles were prefixed with "Kuileot," so this period ultimately became known as the Kuileot Dynasty—although the term "Kuileot" itself might also have been merely a general term for "monarch."

    The Kuileot Dynasty lasted for a considerable period, now generally believed to be around eight hundred years. During this time, agricultural production methods were still at a relatively primitive stage, capable of generating a certain amount of accumulation but unable to provide a stable foundation for the dynasty's rule; barbarian tribes occasionally harassed several remote small city-states, plundering grain, population, and equipment; the accumulation of materials formed some basic exchange but had not yet developed into a trade system. In simple terms, the productivity level of this period did not provide the material foundation required for long-term and stable rule by those in power; although the core political system remained stable, no definitive evidence of smooth succession to the throne has been found, making it impossible to judge whether the regime changes during this period were truly as recorded in the inscriptions; it is also unknown whether there were actual blood relationships between kings or some kind of abdication and inheritance system. This perhaps also sowed the seeds for the dynasty's end.

    The Wrath of the Sea God: HeimetHeimet Dynasty¶

    The name Heimet also originated from one of the ancient city-states in the Trusk region, but the Heimet of the late Kuileot Dynasty was not entirely the same concept as the Heimet of the original city-state era. The original Heimet city was north of Serk Mountain, along the old Pernan River, but that Heimet had been destroyed by floods. It was replaced by the present-day Heimet south of Serk Mountain, still along the Pernan River but near Heimet Bay. By the end of the Kuileot Dynasty, Heimet had already become the largest port in the north. Trusk's colonization of the western coast of the continent was particularly prosperous during this period. Various materials were collected and distributed at Heimet Port, and Heimet's commerce reached unprecedented prosperity, leading to its continuous growth in scale and political status. This was also the economic foundation that ultimately allowed Heimet to seize the opportunity to raise an army.

    The fall of the Kuileot Dynasty was similar to the dynastic changes of many in history, influenced by the cyclical fluctuations of the political situation and agricultural production. According to historical records, the late dynasty may have suffered from sustained large-scale droughts, severely damaging the agricultural economy. At the same time, doubts about the legitimacy of the Kuileot king's bloodline also emerged within the court, but due to the fragmentary nature of related records, specific details are difficult to verify. Under the intertwining of multiple crises, the Kuileot Dynasty quickly fell into comprehensive turmoil and ultimately declined.

    According to several inscriptions from the Heimet period, the turmoil first began in the south. Several border city-states in northern Laprio, which had long-standing conflicts with the Trusk city-state, took advantage of the chaos to invade the southern part of the Trusk Basin. While the Kuileot army was concentrated on dealing with the Laprio people and had no extra troops, the Heimet city lord MataasioksaamenMataasioksaamen raised an army in the name of the sea god to campaign against Kuileot. The Heimet army did not expend much effort to capture half of Trusk's core cities, captured the then-young Kuileot king, and successfully cut off the grain supply from various cities to the Kuileot army. Soon, the Kuileot army disintegrated due to lack of provisions. After consolidating the various forces within Trusk, the Heimet army quickly moved south to repel the Laprio army and probably captured one or two cities along the way.

    It is speculated that Trusk subsequently fell into a complex power struggle: on the surface, the young monarch of the Kuileot family remained the nominal ruler, but actual power was held by Mataasioksaamen. With the defeat, most of the Kuileot family's power had already collapsed. However, within Trusk's core regions, especially the Kuileot city-state, a large number of remnants still existed. The nobles who had previously fought among themselves also united at this time to jointly resist Heimet's rule. Meanwhile, the old nobles who had been suppressed by the Kuileot family also regrouped, forming a force that could not be ignored. In the following years, rebellions frequently broke out within the empire. To maintain rule, the Heimet army had to be stationed long-term in the Trusk Basin for suppression.

    During this transitional period of frequent power struggles, while Mataasioksaamen consolidated his rule and waited for an opportunity to end the Kuileot legal tradition, he also carried out various reforms of the state. Among them, the Heimet-Trusk belief system was established during this period. Mataasioksaamen systematically organized the mythological systems of the various city-states, recompiled the pantheon, and significantly elevated the status of Heimet deities within it. Additionally, he began to improve the tax system to strengthen tax management of merchants traveling between city-states. In terms of law, Mataasioksaamen attempted to implement new legal statutes based on Heimet's original laws but ultimately failed to form a written legal code. In terms of infrastructure construction, he oversaw the construction of the Mataasioksaamen Road connecting the Heimet Strait and the Trusk Basin, and built ports in Heimet and the southern coastal areas of the Trusk Basin. These projects not only laid the foundation for the future expansion of the Trusk Empire to the western coast of the strait but also greatly facilitated the transmission of information and flow of materials between Heimet and various parts of the empire. The above series of measures are collectively referred to by later generations as the "Mataasioksaamen Reforms."

    Mataasioksaamen ultimately did not ascend to the throne of Trusk. Mataasioksaamen's son, Mataasioksaamen II, finally ascended the throne as the son-in-law of the Kuileot family, and the Heimet Dynasty began.

    Building on the foundation of the Kuileot Dynasty, the Heimet Dynasty developed agricultural production methods, largely resolving the contradiction between the growing demand for grain and insufficient grain reserves. In commerce, the Mataasioksaamen Reforms and the construction of the Mataasioksaamen Road provided convenience for the prosperity of commercial activities. A primitive monetary system began to form, and a good balance system between the king and merchants was established. Militarily, Heimet also successfully resisted invasions by the Andon people from the northeast and pirates from the Northern Wastes. The empire's western border continuously expanded until the Anioksait jungle blocked the army's advance. The implementation of legal statutes protected the rights of ordinary people but also to some extent consolidated the interests of the ruling class, laying the groundwork for some subsequent political events in the empire.

    Apart from the above events, not many major nationwide events occurred during the Heimet First Dynasty period. What is worth mentioning might be several major wars against the Andon people and the Laprio people. The Andon people were mountain dwellers in the northwest of the empire, mostly living by fishing and hunting, with simple yet fierce customs. Warriors carried shields and axes, posing a significant threat to the Heimet Dynasty.

    Southern Barbarian Invasion: Novaya Dynasty and Basib Dynasty¶

    The Laprio people in the south did not develop into a systematic state during the early city-state era. The southern land was once too hot and humid for farming, and the Laprio population never achieved effective growth. However, after Heimet counterattacked and nearly flattened several northern states speaking Laprio dialects, the Laprio people began to realize the importance of unity.

    During the several hundred years of the Heimet Dynasty, under the leadership of the wealthiest city-state on the western side of the Laprio Strait, the Laprio people gradually formed a loose confederation of city-states to resist the increasing oppression from the Heimet-Trusk people. There was also a period of relative peace between the two city-state confederations: Trusk people and Laprio people coexisted peacefully at the border, merchants traveled freely between Laprio and Trusk; ships sailed unimpeded in Novaya, and carriages with permits entered and exited the border without any hindrance. After purchasing high-quality iron ore and ironware produced in Trusk, the Laprio people's farming technology developed rapidly, and the amount of cultivated land increased rapidly. According to unearthed inscriptions, there might have been a not-short ice age during the mid-Heimet Dynasty period. Agricultural production in the northern Heimet regime likely stagnated for a time. During this period, Laprio, relying on the hot and humid southern climate, still ensured agricultural production and likely exported large quantities of grain to Trusk. The trade surplus during this period was likely key to the Laprio people ultimately achieving a comprehensive national strength overtake.

    At this time, Trusk began to show signs of over-reliance on grain imports from the Laprio people. The Trusk king was worried about this for a long time. Heimet spent decades dealing with the Laprio people, but at this time, a high phase difference of "snapshot" suddenly appeared. The use of "magic" suddenly became rampant, causing considerable damage and public panic.

    The Novaya War began in the late Heimet Dynasty. The entire conflict lasted about twenty years in total. The first seventeen years mostly involved small-scale conflicts among border nobles, while higher-intensity battles and engagements were concentrated in the final three years. The entire war was almost unremarkable. Both armies were mired in endless logistical quagmires. The Novaya army lacked a good transportation and logistics system, while the Heimet army fundamentally lacked grain and fodder supplies.

    The Novaya Dynasty ultimately captured Heimet. Because they controlled the grain and fodder supply to Heimet-Trusk during this period, it was also difficult for various places in Heimet-Trusk to organize any effective resistance.

    We know very little about the various details of Novaya rule. Perhaps the Novaya Dynasty did not have the habit of recording stories with inscriptions, or perhaps the later Kuileot Second Dynasty destroyed these inscriptions for political purposes. Therefore, there are not many means now to glimpse the ruling structure of the Novaya Dynasty at that time. From some books of the Kuileot Dynasty, we can know that compared to the relatively strict ruling style of the northern regime, the rulers of the Novaya Dynasty mainly pursued a state of non-interference. There were no major reforms within the empire, and the government's main duties shifted to regulating grain and corvée labor. During this period, the aristocratic ethos brought by the Heimet Dynasty also weakened. The Novaya people did not have a formed and closed aristocratic class; the upper echelon was composed of warlords and merchants. During this period, Novaya also did not have a so-called "kingrekui," although according to records from the Kuileot Second Dynasty period, there should have been such a term for the ruler (they directly borrowed the Truskean word "rekui").
