## Fort Yipa

I stood by the sea at the port of Vatsukasem, the wind blowing in gusts. It was winter, the wind was cool, not as fishy as I remembered.

Five years ago in the summer, my mother had just recovered from an illness and suddenly said she wanted to see Vatsukasem. So I explained all the household chores to Uncle Fatuo, rented a large carriage, and set off with my mother. Mother didn’t have much strength, lying limp in a corner of the carriage, eyes closed as if sleeping; she would cough from time to time, and after coughing, she would open her eyes to look at me. Whenever I turned my gaze to her, she would close her eyes again.

After two days of bumping along in the carriage, we reached the port of Heimet. We switched to a boat, and after a week of tossing on the waves, we finally arrived at <ruby>Yipa<rt>Green Land</rt></ruby>. Mother, unusually spirited, insisted on climbing the city wall to see the view. I showed the guard my token, tossed him two guye, and he let us pass.

After walking along the long city wall, we could easily climb the small hill near the fortress. From the hill, we could look out in all directions. I didn’t know where Mother got so much energy to walk so far all at once. The higher we climbed, the happier she became, looking more and more spirited, no longer like a sick person at all. She seemed very familiar with the place, heading straight for the rock that overlooked the entire city.

Before coming to Fort Yipa, I had always thought that Laprio, being so far south, must be a hot and desolate place. But Fort Yipa was actually beautiful, living up to its name “Green Land”—the city faced the sea, backed by mountains, the Krusofa River rushed by not far away, and between these mountains, sea, river, and city, were dotted patches of green, whether trees, shrubs, or farmland.

Unlike the northern fortresses, where residential areas were scattered outside the walls, Fort Yipa was much larger than the northern “fortresses.” Called a fortress, in my eyes it was already a city. The buildings in the city were densely packed, most two or three stories high, mostly wooden structures. Carriages, horses, pedestrians, scattered livestock, and moluo filled the streets.

We reached that rock. Mother pointed to the only white building with a tall tower in the city, telling me that was the lord’s residence of Fort Yipa. Historically, whether it was the Lord of Yipa or the Shepherd of Yipa, they all lived there and usually handled affairs there. I had never seen Mother like this, nor did she usually talk so much. At that moment, I didn’t know what to say, just tilting my head to look at her while nodding like a chicken pecking rice.

She also said, don’t think Fort Yipa looks old and shabby; it’s actually quite sturdy. The rammed earth walls may look poor, but they’re more resistant to battering than the northern stone walls. This was built by Emperor Dezhouhei over fifty years ago. Fifty years ago, Dezhouhei established his foothold here and began his great unification.

I wondered when Mother had started caring so much about these past events, like scholars or Jiaohuzayi. Mother always urged me to go to the Guan’ge or study at Tanka, but I didn’t like it there. Both scholars and Jiaohuzayi seemed lifeless, making one feel utterly unenthusiastic.

At some point, Mother had a somewhat mottled guye in her hand. I felt I had seen it somewhere before. She seemed to realize I was looking at her and the guye, clenched it, looked up at me, hesitated to speak, then lowered her head and hugged her knees. Her gaze should have been toward that white tower, her eyes shifting. Suddenly, she looked like a girl younger than me.

There was a thin spring on the hill, trickling down, passing not far from the rock we stood on. She suddenly said, do you know why Dezhouhei could withstand the Laprio siege here for a full year? She pointed to the river. I thought it might not just be the river. As for many stories about Dezhouhei, at least my teacher harbored many doubts. To me, there were also many hard-to-understand points. But at this moment, how could I argue with her about these senseless stories?

Going down the hill, Mother’s coughing worsened again, uncontrollable for a while. I sat with her on the steps of the gate tower. The soldier on duty was about to go up the wall for patrol, saw us sitting there, and since our clothing didn’t look like that of idlers, he hesitated whether to continue upward. I spoke to him in Truskean, and he seemed confused. So I imitated a few sentences of Novayan I learned from books and asked him to spare us a few more moments.

He seemed to understand, yet not quite, and we stared at each other. Mother felt better by then, and I helped her walk downstairs.

At the bottom, Mother suddenly seemed spirited again but could hardly walk. I called a few laborers from a nearby fish shop and asked the guard for a sedan chair. However, Mother complained the laborers smelled too fishy. Just as I was about to find other laborers, Mother changed her mind, saying smelling fish was fine too, and she hadn’t smelled fish for a long time. But I thought we had just been tossing on a boat for a week; how could she say it had been a long time? Considering Mother might still be ill and had expended a lot of energy climbing up and down today, perhaps her mind was gradually weakening.

Mother got into the sedan chair. Two laborers, one in front and one behind, lifted her. Although Mother disliked their fishy smell, I promised them one guye each, so they happily endured her disdain.

Mother said she wanted to go to Jueying Lou. They didn’t know where that was. I asked the guard, who said it might be Yiying Lou. I told the two laborers in Novayan. The laborers turned around and back. At first, I worried they might jostle Mother off, but strength was their livelihood. I saw Mother sitting steadily in the sedan chair.

Along the way, I saw Mother looked tired, curled up in the sedan chair without speaking. So I used my not-so-fluent Novayan to chat idly with the two laborers. The thinner one in front chattered a lot, but unfortunately, I mostly didn’t understand, only roughly grasping that he seemed to be complimenting me for being wealthy and filial, traveling so far with my mother and taking her to Yiying Lou to enjoy. The big, fat laborer in the back knew a little Truskean but didn’t talk much.

I couldn’t help but become curious about this so-called Yiying Lou. The thin laborer’s eyes seemed to light up when mentioning Yiying Lou. He said the drinks there, he didn’t know what spices were used or how they were brewed. That day, he was lucky to beg a half-cup from a noble guest and still couldn’t forget the fragrance. He also said if the wine was so fragrant, he couldn’t imagine how the dishes were, let alone how beautiful the girls inside must be. He babbled a lot; I didn’t quite understand, just filling in the unclear parts, probably meaning something like that.

We reached a large square with a well and a statue beside it. From afar, I couldn’t see who it was. Around the well and statue were various markets, stalls, and tents: selling grain, livestock, alcohol, straw; I also saw several larger tents with three feathers hanging at the entrance. The fat one said those were slave-selling tents, darkened so slaves wouldn’t know where they were. The thin one said they were foreign merchants selling rare goods. I didn’t know who was right. Between tents, there were at least passages wide enough for two people, wider near larger tents. The flow of people and livestock was bustling, very lively. Past this street seemed to be shops. Unlike the north, where markets had walls and timed management, here, by convention, residential areas and shops were separated.

Mother suddenly woke up in the sedan chair. I knew she didn’t speak Novayan, but she suddenly shouted in pure Krusofa dialect, “Stop, stop!” The thin one stopped immediately; the fat one was still dazed, paused, jolted Mother upward, startling both him and me. Fortunately, he had some skill, exerted strength, and caught Mother steadily.

Mother again said in Krusofa dialect, “Go, go there.” The thin one looked at her pointing at the statue, then looked back at me. I thought, obviously, I was here to accompany Mother, so naturally, I followed her lead. How could this thin one lack such perception? Maybe southerners were like this, much more cautious than people in Turha. Previously, when I went to Turha mountain to deliver a letter for Mother, I also took a sedan chair. The bearers there understood Truskean but never listened carefully to what I said.

We squeezed through the crowded flow of people. Here, people saw laborers carrying a foreigner with another foreigner walking alongside, not particularly surprised. Probably there were many foreigners here; they were used to it.

Approaching the statue, Mother had the sedan chair put down. I helped her forward. The statue was twice my height, likely carved from local stone. The outline was somewhat blurred, unclear when it was erected. Below was a line of small, messy writing. I could only make out two or three parts: something like “First Year of Dacheng, King Tyesuku erected,” the rest in an unknown language, and I didn’t know what “Dacheng” meant.

Mother looked at the statue for a long time, then told me to fetch water from the well. The well was wide, about fitting five or six people standing side by side. It had two windlasses: one connected to a large bucket, one to a thinner tube. I threw the tube down, pulled it up with the windlass, filled the waterskin halfway, and gave it to Mother. I thought she was thirsty, but after getting it, she didn’t drink, just stared at the waterskin blankly.

The fat one then said, “This well is blessed by a god, blessed by that god-child. Drinking it cures all diseases. The old lady will surely recover if she drinks.” I didn’t know who the god-child was. The fat one said this in Truskean. I thought Mother understood, but she didn’t like being called old, nor madam, nor being treated as a patient. I thought Mother would surely be annoyed, but she just looked up, dazedly repeating “cures all diseases” a few times, saying nothing more.

Mother, pale-faced, sat back in the sedan chair. The two laborers lifted it and walked off. I followed unhurriedly behind. At that time, I didn’t understand, still somewhat confused. Why did Mother call me to Vatsukasem? I knew she had probably stayed in Vatsukasem for a while before, but why did we first come to Fort Yipa, delaying these few days?

Seeing Mother slumped in the sedan chair, listless, I remembered the scholars’ descriptions of signs of life, aging, sickness, and death. Thinking of Mother’s recent recovery from serious illness, sometimes spirited, sometimes wilted, perhaps it was a final rally. I couldn’t help but worry greatly, almost wanting to cry. But on the road, with many people around, tears stung my sinuses, held back, unsure if my eyes were red.

Lost in wild thoughts, I didn’t notice that in a blink, Jueying Lou arrived. They put down the sedan chair. I gave the thin and fat laborers one guye each, plus a few copper coins. They clasped hands in thanks, turned, and left. Helping Mother up, I suddenly realized: after they left, how would I carry the sedan chair? I didn’t mind carrying it, but how could one person do it alone? I’d have to find another way later.

Jueying Lou was four stories high, probably the only building in the city almost as tall as the lord’s residence. I had seen it from the square earlier. This wasn’t allowed in the north nowadays. The first floor was a dining hall. Usually, the second floor of an inn would be lodging, but in a large inn like Jueying Lou, the second floor was also filled with tables.

I couldn’t help but wonder: Fort Yipa plus a few nearby towns totaled no more than ten thousand people, and the surrounding areas were mostly rural villages. How could there be materials and labor to build such a large building? Also, Fort Yipa was built so grandly, rare even in the north. Could Emperor Dezhouhei really have had magic?

Entering the building, it was cold and quiet, starkly different from the bustling market outside, far from what I imagined.

Mother sighed upon seeing it. Had she been here before? She had lived in Vatsukasem, only a day’s journey from here, so it was normal to have visited. Uncle Fatuo said she used to be very wealthy, but neither of them told me what Mother did before or what my maternal grandfather’s family did.

Mother found a table by the window and sat down. The decor here was indeed peculiar, surpassing many taverns I’d seen in the north. The table sides were inlaid with some unknown metal; whether with metal or not, they were carved with extremely intricate patterns of birds, beasts, exotic flowers, and plants.

Mother didn’t seem surprised, as if she had seen these things before. She raised her hand to call a waiter, asking what dishes they had.

The waiter looked lazy, saying the chef wasn’t on duty yet, no dishes, only pastries and tea.

She said, “Alright, then bring whatever.”

In a few minutes, the waiter brought a plate of unfamiliar pastries. They tasted like glutinous rice skin with sesame filling, deep-fried. I asked the waiter, who said they were called mogeqi.

The tea was far worse, not as good as the tribute tea from Anioksait we drank in the capital.

With something to eat, Mother became more spirited. She picked up a mogeqi, took a bite, oil staining her lips. Her teeth were starting to weaken, and being ill, chewing this sticky pastry seemed somewhat difficult.

She ate one, looked like she wanted another but seemed unable. She said she had always liked this pastry but had forgotten its name, so during all those years in Heimet and Zhaoxiasi, she never took me to eat it.

## Jueying Lou

After drinking two cups of tea, I gradually felt the need to relieve myself and called a waiter to lead me to the toilet. Surprisingly, the toilet here had a bamboo running water system, ingeniously drawing mountain spring water as a source.

While washing my hands, I felt the building shaking slightly and smelled a strange fragrance, particularly prominent amid the toilet’s unpleasant odor. A subtle dizziness enveloped me. I didn’t know what was happening, instinctively grabbing the bamboo used for water, but my hand slipped, and I fell, everything turning black.

I don’t know how long I was unconscious. When I woke, I heard the clashing of metal, and fear gripped me. Staggering, I rushed to the hall, seeing tables and chairs scattered everywhere from a fight. I looked at the spot where Mother and I had been sitting, only to see a woman standing there, wielding a full-length spear so swiftly it seemed impenetrable. Four armored soldiers surrounded her, two already lying on the ground.

Although the woman’s spearplay dazzled me, under the assault of four fully armored soldiers, she was beginning to show signs of strain. I didn’t know what had happened or where Mother was, unsure what to do, just standing there watching the fight.

The woman seemed to notice me during a lull in the battle. The moment our eyes met, she threw something at me. I couldn’t see clearly, instinctively caught it—a package. The soldiers’ gazes followed the object toward me. They wore iron masks, and I shivered, instinctively running outside. Two soldiers continued engaging the woman, while two chased after me.

I rushed out the door, realizing this building wasn’t Jueying Lou, and this city wasn’t Yipa. Where was I? But I had no time to ponder, just running forward desperately. After a few moments, my legs grew sore, slowing momentarily. Glancing back hastily, I saw the two soldiers also lying on the ground. The woman pulled her spear from the gap in a soldier’s helmet, wiped it on her sleeve.

I stopped. I knew if this woman wanted to kill me, I probably couldn’t escape. Only then did I notice the city I was in. I stood on a broad road, surrounded by two- or three-story wooden buildings like Jueying Lou, lining the street. The street was quite busy, filled with pedestrians, carriages, and horses.

Our fight had attracted considerable attention. I looked at the woman; she looked at me, her eyes revealing a murderous aura. I quickly took out the package I had instinctively tucked into my robe and threw it back to her.

She unwrapped it, glanced inside, slung the spear on her back, and ran off. I looked around; all the writing was unfamiliar. Nearby came the sound of armored soldiers’ plates rubbing. Panicked, unsure where to go, I steeled myself and ran after her.

She ran dozens of feet along the broad road, then ducked into a side alley. I followed closely behind. She seemed focused on escaping, not noticing me. I strained to keep up in these unfamiliar, twisting alleys. But after about five or six turns, she still disappeared.

I hid behind a haystack for a long time. The sky gradually darkened, the surroundings grew quiet. A black-and-white bird flew over the haystack I was hiding in, called once. I felt this was some kind of sign.

I emerged from the haystack, looking around. The city seemed especially unfamiliar in the dark. Fortunately, the moonlight wasn’t too dim, allowing me to see the way forward. The city’s roads were crooked and twisted. Advancing in the night, I didn’t know exactly where I was going, just roughly heading in the same direction, wanting to leave the city first. The authorities might still be pursuing me and that woman.

As I walked, the soil underfoot seemed to soften, soon turning into stone slabs. I thought if the people here had habits similar to Truskeans, I must be near the river. Soon enough, I saw mottled reflections on the ground—a river about a dozen zhang wide.

Walking along the river for a while, I found a boat. The boat’s shape was different from Truskean ones; I had never seen Truskeans make boat bottoms so flat.
