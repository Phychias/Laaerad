# Laprio Language

!!! Abstract "Preface"

Laprio is a language spoken by the [Laprio](../../地理/拉普里奥/index.md) people in the southern frontier of the Empire. It is now gradually declining, with many regions shifting to a Laprio dialect of Truskean.

---

## 1 Phonology

Ancient Laprio has a percussive, crystalline auditory quality: crisp, powerful, and distinctly bounded.

### 1.1 Vowels

Ancient Laprio possesses **5 basic monophthongs** and **5 core diphthongs**.

#### 1.1.1 Monophthongs

Vowels maintain stable quality in both stressed and unstressed syllables; **reduction is strictly prohibited**.

| **Front** | **Central** | **Back** |           |
| --------------- | ----------------- | -------------- | --------- |
| **Close**   | **i** /i/         |                | **u** /u/ |
| **Mid**    | **e** /e/         |                | **o** /o/ |
| **Open**    |                   | **a** /a/      |           |

#### 1.1.2 Diphthongs

Diphthongs typically originate from the **breaking** of Proto-Laaeradian long vowels.

| **Orthography** | **IPA** | **Etymological Correspondence (PL)**    |
| ------- | ------- | ---------------- |
| **au**  | /au/    | $\bar{a} \to au$ |
| **eo**  | /eo/    | $\bar{e} \to eo$ |
| **ie**  | /ie/    | $\bar{i} \to ie$ |
| **oi**  | /oi/    | $\bar{o} \to oi$ |
| **ia**  | /ia/    | Suffix Specialization             |

### 1.3 Syllable & Prosody

#### 1.3.1 Syllable Structure

Ancient Laprio allows complex onsets but maintains relatively clean codas.

- **Maximum Template**: $(C)(C)V(V)(C)$
    
- **Consonant Clusters**:
    
    - Permits **"stop + liquid"** combinations word-initially: **Kl-** (_Klau_), **Pr-** (_Prakit_), **Tr-** (_Trot_), **Kr-** (_Kreat_).
        
    - Permits **"s + stop"** combinations: **Sp-** (_Spiom_), **St-**.
Good, here is the second part: **Morphology**.

This chapter is the most complex and fascinating aspect of Ancient Laprio. It is the watershed distinguishing "elegant speech" from "vernacular," establishing the language's inflectional characteristic of a **"frame structure"** (prefix for class + suffix for case).

## 2 Morphology

!!! Info "Core Logic: Frame Structure"

Words in Ancient Laprio—especially nouns—rarely appear bare. They are typically encased within a strict grammatical framework:

$$\mathbf{Prefix} \text{ (Prefix: Class)} + \mathbf{ROOT} \text{ (Root)} + \mathbf{Suffix} \text{ (Suffix: Case)}$$

This structure grants the language high stability and a sense of ritual.

### 2.1 Nouns

#### 2.1.1 Gender System

The gender system in Ancient Laprio is not based on biological sex (feminine/masculine) but on **spatial perception** and **theological essence**. This is key to understanding the language's worldview.

- **Li Class (Li-Class / Civic / Proximal)**
    
    - **Meaning**: Mundane, proximal, man-made, concrete, controllable, social.
        
    - **Prefix**: **Li-** (contracts to **L'** before vowels)
        
    - **Typical Categories**: Humans, occupations, tools, buildings, livestock, small concrete objects.
        
- **Na Class (Na-Class / Natural / Distal)**
    
    - **Meaning**: Natural, distal, sacred, grand, uncontrollable, abstract.
        
    - **Prefix**: **Na-** (contracts to **N'** before vowels)
        
    - **Typical Categories**: Natural elements (sea/fire/wind), celestial bodies, Johuzai, abstract concepts (love/death/time), large geographical features.
        

#### 2.1.2 Declension System

To support the formal **SOV** word order (where the object precedes the verb), suffixes must clearly distinguish subject and object.

| **Case**  | **Function**      | **Li Class Suffix (Sg/Pl)** | **Na Class Suffix (Sg/Pl)** | **Auditory Feature**                |
| -------------- | ----------- | ---------------- | ---------------- | ----------------------- |
| **Nominative (Nom)**   | Syntactic Subject        | **-os / -i**     | **-as / -ae**    | **-s** ending, crisp and powerful, indicates agent. |
| **Accusative (Acc)**   | Direct Object        | **-on / -in**    | **-an / -aen**   | **-n** ending, with a sense of continuation, indicates recipient.  |
| **Dative/Oblique (Dat)** | Indirect Object/Instrument/After Preposition | **-om / -im**    | **-am / -aem**   | **-m** ending, closed sound, indicates stasis or endpoint.  |
| **Genitive (Gen)**   | _Possessive_       | **-is**          | **-is**          | Usually used with the preposition **Ti**.       |

> **Declension Demonstration:**
> 
> - **Root**: `Kom` (person - Li class)
>     
>     - **Subject**: _Li-Kom**os**_ (that person...)
>         
>     - **Object**: _Li-Kom**on**_ ([to] that person...)
>         
> - **Root**: `Falar` (sea - Na class)
>     
>     - **Subject**: _Na-Falar**as**_ (that sea...)
>         
>     - **Object**: _Na-Falar**an**_ ([to] that sea...)
>         

---

### 2.2 Adjectives

Adjectives in Ancient Laprio are **dependent elements**; they must "attach" to a noun and phonetically mimic the noun's morphology.

#### 2.2.1 Word Order and Concord

1. **Position**: Always placed **after** the noun.
    
2. **Rule**: Adjectives do not take a prefix but must take the suffix corresponding to the noun's **case, number, and class**.
    

$$\text{Structure:} [\text{CLS-NOUN-CASE}] + [\text{ADJ-CASE}]$$

**Example:**

- **Li Class Modification** (using `Sykio` sword + `Bord` straight):
    
    - Subject: **Li-Sykios Bordos** (that straight sword...)
        
    - Object: **Li-Sykion Bordon** ([to] that straight sword...)
        
- **Na Class Modification** (using `Falar` sea + `Klafias` grand):
    
    - Subject: **Na-Falaras Klafias** (that grand sea...)
        
    - Object: **Na-Falaran Klafian** ([to] that grand sea...)
        

#### 2.2.2 Comparative and Superlative

- **Comparative**: Root + **-ior**
    
    - _Klau-ior_ (bigger)
        
- **Superlative**: Root + **-issim**
    
    - _Klau-issim_ (greatest/biggest)
        

---

### 2.3 Verbs

Verbs are the **anchor** of the sentence, located at the end. Their morphology primarily marks **tense** and **aspect**, and stems typically end in a consonant.

#### 2.3.1 Tense-Aspect Suffix Chart

| **Category**    | **Suffix**      | **Meaning** | **Example (Root: Kem- to exist)**                                          |
| --------- | ----------- | ------ | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Present** | **-a / -e** | States current condition | _Li-Komios **Keme**._ (The person exists.)<br>_Na-Falaras **Kema**._ (The sea exists.) |
| **Past** | **-it**     | Narrates past fact | _**Kemit**._ (Existed.)<br>_Note: -it is the most common narrative suffix._                     |
| **Future** | **-niat**   | Prediction or plan  | _**Kemniat**._ (Will exist.)                                         |
| **Progressive**   | **-ko**     | Ongoing action   | _**Kemko**._ (Is existing/continuing.)                                      |
| **Perfective**   | **-pe**     | Action completed  | _**Kempe**._ (Has existed.)                                          |

#### 2.3.2 Negation

The negative particle **Non** exists independently and is placed immediately before the verb.

- _Li-Komios Non Pakit._ (That person did not kill.)
    

#### 2.3.3 Verbal Nominalization

The suffix **-da** is used to turn a verb into a noun (typically classified as Li class, as a kind of "action fact").

- _Prak-_ (to buy) $\to$ **Li-Prakda** (the act of buying/transaction).

Pronouns retain an ancient form, taking no prefix but having case changes.

| ---------------- | ------------ | ------------ | ------------ | ------- |

### 2.5 Particles & Prepositions

Prepositions are placed before the noun, requiring the noun to take the **dative/oblique case (-om/-am)**.

- **Ti** (of): Indicates genitive relationship.
    
    - _Iklis **ti** Sauh_ (light of the moon).
        
- **Em** (in): Indicates location.
    
    - _**Em** Li-Seream_ (in the house).
        
- **Tra** (between):
    
    - _**Tra** L'Iklisam_ (among the radiance).
        
- **Et** (and): Conjunction.

## 3. Syntax

### 3.1 Basic Word Order

Laprio is a strictly **SOV (Subject-Object-Verb)** language.

Unlike the "linear flow" of Truskean (SVO), Laprio sentence structure possesses strong **enclosure** and **suspense**. The verb, as the "seal" of the sentence, must be placed at the end, marking the conclusion of a complete thought unit.

> Syntactic Formula:
> 
> $$\mathbf{S} \text{ (Subject-Nom)} + \mathbf{O} \text{ (Object-Acc)} + \mathbf{V} \text{ (Verb-Tense)}$$

**Example:**

> Li-Komios Li-Sykion Prakit.
> 
> Li-person-Nom Li-sword-Acc buy-Past
> 
> Translation: That person bought that sword.

Cultural Metaphor:

This word order reflects the Laprio mindset—first stating the participants (who and whom), and only finally revealing what happened (the action). This is particularly important in trade negotiations and theological debates, as it forces the listener to patiently hear the entire sentence.

---

### 3.2 Noun Phrases

Noun phrases internally follow a **head-initial** principle. The core noun comes first, acting as the foundation of the phrase; modifying elements (adjectives, genitives) follow closely, sculpting it.

#### 3.2.1 Adjective Modification

Adjectives are placed after the noun and strictly follow **case concord**.

> **Structure**: `[CLS-NOUN-CASE]` + `[ADJ-CASE]`
 
**Example**:

- **Li-Sykios Bordos** (that straight sword - subject form)
    
- **Li-Sykion Bordon** (that straight sword - object form)
    

#### 3.2.2 The Genitive Structure

To indicate possession, the preposition **Ti** (of) is used to connect. The possessor typically takes the **genitive suffix (-is)**.

> **Structure**: `[possessed]` + **Ti** + `[possessor-is]`

**Example**:

> na-iyelas ti sausam
> 
> Na-radiance of Na-moon-Gen
> 
> Translation: the moon's radiance / moonlight

---

### 3.3 Prepositional Phrases

Prepositions (e.g., _Em_ in, _Tra_ between) are placed before the noun. The noun following a preposition must be in the **dative/oblique case**, ending in **-m**.

> **Structure**: `Prep` + `[CLS-NOUN-Oblique(-m)]`

**Example**:

> em asot-fiorom
> 
> in era-ancient-Obl
> 
> Translation: in the ancient era

---

### 3.4 Sentence Expansion

When a sentence contains adverbials (time/place), Ancient Laprio tends to **front the adverbial (topic-fronting)**, placing it before the subject as the background framework for the entire sentence.

> Expansion Formula:
> 
> $$\mathbf{Adv} \text{ (Adverbial)} + \mathbf{S} \text{ (Subject)} + \mathbf{O} \text{ (Object)} + \mathbf{V} \text{ (Verb)}$$

**Sentence Analysis**:

> **"Em Na-Falaram, Li-Komios Na-Sohusan Spiomit."**
> 
> 1. **em na-falaram**: (Adverbial) In that sea (_Falar-am_ Oblique).
>     
> 2. **li-komios**: (Subject) That person.
>     
> 3. **na-sohusan**: (Object) That Johuz (_Sohus-an_ Accusative).
>     
> 4. **Spiomit**: (Predicate) Saw (_Spiom_ to see + _it_ Past).
>     
> 
> **Translation**: In the great sea, the person saw the Johuz.

---

## 3.5 Interrogatives

### 3.5.1 Yes/No Questions

The interrogative particle **An** is placed at the beginning of the sentence, with the verb remaining at the end.

> an li-komios li-sykion prakit?
> 
> [Q] that person that sword bought?
> 
> Translation: Did that person buy that sword?

### 3.5.2 Wh-Questions

Interrogative pronouns (who/what/where) are typically moved to the **beginning** of the sentence, with the verb still at the end.

- **makis** (who-Nom)
    
- **maon** (what-Acc)
    
- **makar** (where)
    

> maon li-komios prakit?
> 
> what-Acc that person bought?
> 
> Translation: What did that person buy?

---

## 3.6 Negation & Imperative

### 3.6.1 Negation

The negative adverb **nos** exists independently and is placed **immediately before** the verb.

> li-komios li-sykion nos prakit.
> 
> that person that sword not buy.

### 3.6.2 Imperative

The imperative mood is realized through changes in the verb suffix, often with the subject omitted.

- **Strong Command (Li Class)**: Suffix **-tas** (Go...!)
    
- **Soft Command (Na Class)**: Suffix **-as** (May...)
    

> Prak-tas! (Buy!) —— Said to a merchant.
> 
> Sof-as. (Love.) —— A priest's blessing.

---

## 3.7 Complex Sentences

### 3.7.1 Compound Sentences

Use the conjunctions **e** (and) or **ben** (but/however).

> Li-Komios Kemit, ben Na-Sohusas nos Kemit.
> 
> The person existed, but the Johuz did not exist.

Due to post-nominal adjectives, relative clauses are also placed after the antecedent, introduced by the relative pronoun **Ke** (that/which). The clause internally still maintains SOV word order.
