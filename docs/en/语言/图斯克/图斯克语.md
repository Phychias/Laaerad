# Truskean

**Truskean** is the official language of the Trusk Empire, with its standard form based on **Strait Truskean** (i.e., the Heimet dialect). This book primarily documents the form officially adopted around the 175th year of the Imperial Calendar, in the second year of Emperor Tyesuku Laiswi's reign. The use of Truskean covers vast areas including Heimet, Tyesuku, Turha, Andon, and Greater Laprio, forming a **dialect continuum**.

## 1 Introduction

### 1.1 📜 Historical and Research Background

The compilation of this book was facilitated by the efforts of the third emperor of the Tyesuku Dynasty — **Tyesuku Dezhohai III**. Inspired by revelations from the Spirit Language, he organized excavations of numerous ancient sites, including Glömezher steles in Mistnatila, Mibion obelisks in the Laprio mountains, and various statues from ancient Trusk. Subsequently, he founded the **Royal Academy**, establishing a dedicated institution to provide funding and manpower for the systematic study of various phenomena within the Empire.

During the fifteen years of Dezhohai III's reign, the Academy focused on deciphering the scripts on these relics. Their achievements formed the foundation of our current understanding of **Old Truskean** and even **Proto-Laaeradian**. After the emperor's death, the Academy's funding was reduced, research on ancient sites slowed, and efforts shifted towards organizing existing materials and studying the dialects of various Trusk regions through comparative linguistics, which became an important basis for this book.

After the current emperor **Laiswi** ascended the throne, he ordered the compilation of a series of books documenting conditions across the Empire. Coinciding with the scholar **Da Jiaohu Zayi**'s attempts to expand research on the Spirit Language, the Academy was commissioned to write this monograph on Truskean to assist in related studies.

### 1.2 🗺️ Dialect Distribution

Truskean dialects can be broadly divided into **coastal dialects** and **mountain dialects**, with mountain dialects further subdivided into eastern and western.

- **Coastal Dialects**: Distributed along the coast of the Novaya Sea, including the Heimet dialect. Their vocabulary system is highly unified, but there are regional differences in segmental pronunciation.
- **Western Mountain Dialects**: Cover the continuous mountainous region from Andon in the north to Zimiak in the south. They have strong internal consistency, forming a relatively coherent dialect continuum.
- **Eastern Mountain Dialects**: Divided into northern and southern branches. The northern mountainous region is adjacent to Glömezher settlements and is significantly influenced by Glömezher, featuring distinctive pronunciation and vocabulary. The southern dialect has fused with local indigenous languages, possessing many unique vocabulary and grammatical phenomena.

### 1.3 🏛️ Language Status and Usage

In regions of the Empire not dominated by Truskean ethnic groups, a **bilingual system** is often implemented:
- The original language of the Laprio region has gradually declined; contemporary Laprio is closer to a dialect of Truskean.
- In Glömezher regions, due to conservative forces resisting Truskification and limited communication between tribes, a stronger state of bilingualism persists, with both the native language and Truskean being used.

Overall, those who master **Strait Truskean (Heimet dialect)** can communicate smoothly in most wealthy, densely populated areas of the Empire. Comprehension difficulties may be encountered in Laprio and the southeastern mountains, but in towns within Glömezher regions, Andon, Bagunalo, and other places, one can still find guides or inn staff proficient in the official language.

## 2 Phonology

### 2.1 Segment Inventory

The **phonology** of Truskean is quite simple:

Standard Truskean has only five basic **vowel** segments. Among them, a and o have nasalized variants, but in some dialects, i and e may also nasalize near nasal consonants. There are many diphthongs, which, despite absolute phonemic differences, can still be viewed as movement between two vowel phonemes.

Standard Truskean has a total of 24 **consonant** segments, as noted in the table below. Overall, it is relatively symmetrical, with a regular opposition between aspirated and unaspirated sounds, but lacks voiced stops; the palato-alveolar series is actually a palatalization of alveolar sounds before the front vowel i, not expressed in the orthography.

### 2.2 Phonotactics

During word derivation and inflection, various phoneme **clusters** may occur. Here is a brief description of common situations in Truskean.

#### 2.2.1 Vowel Clusters
The rules for **vowel** clusters in Truskean are simple:
When two short vowels cluster, if a corresponding diphthong exists, it is pronounced as that diphthong; if no corresponding diphthong exists, the first vowel is taken, and the duration of the second vowel is usually left empty.
If a diphthong and a short vowel cluster, the corresponding triphthong is prioritized; if no corresponding triphthong exists, then if the short vowel comes first, the first vowel of the diphthong is taken and contracted according to the rules for short vowel clusters; if the diphthong comes first, the monophthong is dropped.
If caused by derivation or inflection, it is written in the orthography as the contracted form; if it's an inter-word cluster, pronunciation follows the rules but does not affect spelling.

#### 2.2.2 Consonant Clusters
For **consonant** clusters in Truskean, the general principle is forward assimilation, meaning that when consonants of the same place or manner of articulation cluster, they tend to assimilate forward. Of course, there are various exceptions, and specifics also vary according to different dialects.

### 2.3 Stress

**Stress** usually falls on the **first syllable** of the stem. If the prefix is of the weak or monosyllabic type, stress typically remains on the first syllable of the core root.

## 3 Morphology
### 3.1 Verbs

This section details the morphological inflection system of standard Truskean verbs. Truskean verbs exhibit typical high agglutination characteristics. Their morphological changes are not only a means of rich word formation but also the core mechanism for constructing syntactic frameworks. The operation of verb morphology follows a strict hierarchical logic: components closer to the root tend to change the basic semantic meaning of the word (derivation); components farther from the root tend to anchor the verb in a specific syntactic context through categories like tense, aspect, and mood (inflection).

#### 3.1.1 Morphological Structure Overview

The morphological structure of Truskean verbs can be seen as a concentric hierarchical system centered on the root. Its basic framework follows the linear sequence of **prefix + root + suffix**:

$$\mathbf{Prefixes} \text{ (Subject/Internal)} + \mathbf{ROOT} \text{ (Root)} + \mathbf{Suffixes} \text{ (Object/External)}$$

Within this framework, each affix occupies a fixed syntactic slot. The complete generative sequence is as follows:

$$\mathbf{[P_1 Negation]} + \mathbf{[P_2 Voice/Valency Reduction]} + \mathbf{ROOT} + \mathbf{[S_1 Valency Increase]} + \mathbf{[S_2 Aspect/Manner]} + \mathbf{[S_3 Mood/Tense]}$$

The functions of each level are defined as follows:

1. **Prefix Zone (Internal Domain)**:
    
    - **$P_1$ Negation**: Located outermost, logically negates the entire verb concept.
        
    - **$P_2$ Voice/Valency Reduction**: Handles the subject relationship of the action and valency reduction (e.g., passive, reflexive), changing the nature of the subject's role.
        
2. **Root (ROOT)**: Carries the core semantic concept.
    
3. **Suffix Zone (External Domain)**:
    
    - **$S_1$ Valency Increase (Derivational Layer)**: The most tightly bound derivational affix, introduces new core arguments (e.g., causer, beneficiary), directly changing the verb's valency structure.
        
    - **$S_2$ Aspect/Manner (Lexical Layer)**: Describes the internal manner or state of the action's progression (e.g., thorough, rapid), modifying the nature of the action itself.
        
    - **$S_3$ Mood/Tense (Inflectional Layer)**: The outermost syntactic marker, responsible for marking mood (subjunctive) and tense (future), completing the grammatical connection of the verb to the sentence.
        

#### 3.1.2 Valency System

**Valency** refers to the number of core arguments (subject, object, etc.) a verb can govern. It is the cornerstone of Truskean verb classification:

- **Zero-valent verbs**: Have no logical arguments, mainly used to describe natural phenomena (e.g., "to rain").
    
- **Monovalent verbs (intransitive)**: Govern only one **agent** argument (subject).
    
- **Divalent verbs (transitive)**: Govern an **agent** (subject) and a **patient** (object).
    
- **Trivalent verbs (ditransitive)**: Govern an agent, patient, and **recipient/beneficiary**.
    

#### 3.1.3 Prefix Zone: Voice and Negation

Prefixes are primarily responsible for "subtraction" operations on the action (negation or argument reduction) and adjustment of the subject role.

|**Slot**|**Category**|**Affix**|**Name**|**Function/Semantics**|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|**P1**|**Negation**|**a**|Lexical Negation|Constructs antonym concepts, directly negates action or state.|
|**P2**|**Voice (Valency Reduction)**|**kwa**|Passive Voice|Demotes agent, promotes patient to subject, valency -1.|
|||**zi**|Reflexive Voice|Agent is also patient, action returns to self, valency -1.|
|||**ma**|Reciprocal Voice|Multiple agents act as patients to each other, emphasizes mutual participation, valency -1.|
