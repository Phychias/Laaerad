<ruby>Tamorge<rt>Tamorge</rt></ruby> (828 BC – 771 BC) was a monarch of the Heimet First Dynasty, reigning from 815 BC to 771 BC. His reign is often regarded as a crucial period in Heimet history for the centralization of imperial power and the establishment of the civil service system. He is widely considered one of the most outstanding politicians of that dynasty.

## 🎭 Personality

Tamorge had a deep, reserved, and highly controlling personality. His core achievement lies in ending the prolonged interference by maternal relatives in state affairs, achieving an unprecedented centralization of imperial power, and initially establishing a civil service system directly serving the emperor. Through a series of meticulous administrative reforms, he successfully transformed traditional aristocratic privileges into bureaucratic positions, thereby consolidating central authority.

## 📜 Biography

### 🕵️ The Patient Early Years
After ascending the throne as a child, Tamorge spent a long period caught between the powerful minister Notsot and Prince Yepken. During the first decade of his reign, he maintained a silent and reticent image, allowing the faction of maternal relatives and the prince's forces to attack each other. Meanwhile, he secretly cultivated a group of loyal commoner scholars and merchants in the lower districts of the imperial capital, Hasit, accumulating strength for his future personal rule.

### 📚 The Rise of the Civil Service System
Upon assuming personal rule, Tamorge did not resort directly to violent purges. Instead, he implemented a series of intricate and systematic administrative reforms. He dismantled the hereditary privileges of the aristocratic class into numerous, finely divided administrative positions, and appointed commoners or citizens he personally selected and promoted to key posts. This process was later termed the "bloodless purge" by historians, effectively dismantling the control of traditional families over court politics.

### 👑 The Peak of Power
By the late period of his reign, the Heimet Empire had developed a precisely functioning bureaucratic machine, allowing the reach of imperial power to penetrate every corner of the empire. Tamorge thus became the first emperor in Heimet history who did not need to rely on the favor of traditional family elders, laying the administrative foundation for centralized authority for the following centuries.
